## 1. Introduction

Coffee is one of Kenya's most storied exports — prized globally for its bright acidity and complex flavor, and, closer to home, woven into the livelihoods of more than 700,000 smallholder farming households across the country's central and eastern highlands. For many of these farmers, coffee is not simply a crop but a generational inheritance: land, trees, and knowledge passed down through families who have depended on the harvest for income, education, and stability. My own family's ties to smallholder coffee farming in Kenya are what first drew me to this research — not as an abstract dataset, but as a question with real stakes for people I know.

Over the past several decades, however, Kenyan coffee has been in visible decline. National yield fell by roughly 23% between 1990 and 2024, and coffee's share of Kenya's export earnings has fallen even more dramatically — from a high of roughly 40% in the mid-1980s to a small fraction of that today [figure to be verified]. Climate change is often cited as a primary culprit, and it is a reasonable first guess: coffee is a notoriously climate-sensitive crop, and East Africa has experienced measurable warming in recent decades.

But the data tell a more complicated story than "climate change is destroying Kenyan coffee." Over the same 35-year period examined in this study, precipitation and humidity in Kenya's coffee-growing regions actually *increased* significantly — the opposite of the "hotter and drier" narrative that often accompanies discussions of climate change and African agriculture. If wetter conditions were the whole story, coffee yield should have improved, not collapsed. This divergence is the starting puzzle of this research: **if climate alone does not explain Kenya's declining coffee yield, what does — and how much of the decline can be attributed to climate at all?**

This paper addresses that question in two parts. First, using 35 years (1990–2024) of merged climate and agricultural data across Kenya's major coffee-growing regions, I build and test a series of statistical models to isolate the specific climate variables — temperature, precipitation, humidity, and solar radiation — that have a measurable relationship with coffee yield, and to quantify how much of the yield decline they can explain. Second, informed by the portion of the decline that climate cannot explain, I situate these findings within the broader economic and historical context of Kenya's coffee sector, including the 1989 collapse of the International Coffee Agreement and the market liberalization that followed — a structural shift with its own, distinctly human, fingerprints on the data.

The result is not a simple story of climate destroying an industry, nor a dismissal of climate's role. It is a more precise one: temperature and sunlight — not rainfall — are the climate variables that matter most for Kenyan coffee yield, and even accounting for them, roughly half of the year-to-year variation in yield remains unexplained by weather alone. That unexplained half is where the more interesting, more human part of this story begins.
